# v0.7.4 Failure Study: What An Organoid-Industry Research Run Exposed

[简体中文](v0.7.4-organoid-failure-study.zh-CN.md)

> Public case study. This document summarizes a private dogfooding run and the external fact-checking that followed. It does not publish the raw workspace, transcripts, full source list, local paths, private files, or unreleased operational material.

## One-Line Conclusion

An organoid-industry research run produced a readable industry brief with a source appendix and a complete process surface. External fact-checking later found multiple fact-calibration problems. It is not a suitable showcase for MABW output quality, but it is a useful failure study for MABW's process-accountability claim:

> With direct LLM drafting, errors usually remain only in the final text. With MABW, errors leave a propagation path.

The external reviewer was **ChatGPT 5.5 Thinking Max**. It reviewed the MABW output against public sources and also recorded corrections to some of its own review mistakes. That matters: a stronger model is not a complete answer. The next problem to solve is source-to-claim support calibration as an auditable, graded, reviewable mechanism.

## Run Object

The task was an organoid-industry research brief covering policy and regulation, industry dynamics, research and technology, financing, and commercialization directions.

The MABW run produced the full workflow surface:

- source pack;
- candidate claims;
- screened candidates;
- Claim Ledger;
- audited brief;
- quality gate report;
- finalize report;
- delivery brief;
- source appendix.

The report was not an unsourced text. The failure was subtler and closer to real business risk: **there were sources, but the sources did not always support every qualifier in the final claim. A fact could be partly true but attached to the wrong source, written with excessive certainty, merged into the wrong claim, or over-certified by a secondary summary.**

## Main Findings

### 1. FDA Section: Support Strength Was Overstated

The brief described FDA signals around NAMs, WoE, and fit-for-purpose methods as if organoids had been explicitly accepted by oncology-specific guidance as a source of nonclinical safety data.

A more careful statement would be:

> FDA is promoting the use of WoE and fit-for-purpose NAMs as supportive evidence in selected drug categories and is exploring reductions in some animal toxicology studies. Organoids / MPS can enter the regulatory conversation as NAM tools, but the public materials reviewed here do not support saying that organoids have been explicitly accepted as a nonclinical safety-data source.

Failure type: support-strength inflation.

### 2. China Policy Section: Conference News Became National-Plan Fact

The brief turned university conference news discussing the "15th Five-Year Plan" context into a statement that organoids and organ-on-chip technology had already entered official national planning.

A safer statement would be:

> University and medical-research systems are discussing organoids and organ-on-chip technology in the context of the 15th Five-Year Plan. Whether they have entered official national planning documents requires confirmation from official plans or special policy documents.

Failure type: source authority inflation.

### 3. EU / EMA Section: Valid Main Fact And Unverified Sub-Claims Were Merged

The main fact around the European Commission's animal-testing phase-out roadmap was broadly valid. But sub-claims around EMA / OoC strategic priorities, reflection papers, and global regulatory clusters require separate checks. The brief merged different policy entities, source tiers, and certainty levels into one narrative paragraph.

Failure type: claim conflation.

### 4. Korea Case: Facts May Be True, But Attribution Was Mismatched

Technology transfer, IND, Phase 1, indication, patient count, and "world-first" wording were merged into one Claim Ledger entry, with one technology-transfer source carrying too many sub-conclusions.

Correct handling would split the item into separate claims:

- technology transfer;
- IND;
- Phase 1;
- patient count;
- indication;
- "world-first" wording.

Failure type: attribution mismatch + claim conflation.

### 5. Market-Size Section: Secondary Forecasts Became Core Evidence

The brief used market-size forecasts as an industry-judgment anchor without reaching a strong enough original report page, methodology, or publication basis.

A safer statement would be:

> Third-party industry media summaries indicate expectations of high growth in the organoid market. Because the original report page and methodology were not verified, specific market-size numbers should not be used as core judgment evidence.

Failure type: unverified forecast certification.

## How The Errors Propagated

This was not a single-point failure. It was a propagation chain:

```text
source summary
  -> candidate claims
  -> screened candidates
  -> Claim Ledger
  -> audited brief
  -> final delivery
```

The key weakness was between source and claim:

- LLM / WebSearch summaries were treated as high-confidence evidence.
- Source entries lacked raw excerpts, `retrieved_at`, `source_tier`, and excerpt hashes.
- Multiple sub-facts were compressed into one claim.
- Labels such as `supported` did not distinguish explicit support, directional support, partial support, summary support, and attribution mismatch.
- Freshness lacked an authority axis, so fresh secondary summaries could outweigh older but more authoritative primary sources.

MABW v0.7.x can trace the propagation chain. It cannot automatically prove that each semantic support relation is valid.

## What MABW Got Right

This run did not prove output-quality improvement or factual correctness. It did prove several process-level properties:

- Errors could be traced back through source summary, candidate, Claim Ledger, audited brief, and delivery layers.
- The problem did not disappear into the final text; intermediate artifacts remained available for review.
- Failures could be classified as support-strength inflation, authority inflation, claim conflation, attribution mismatch, and forecast over-certification.
- The external reviewer made and corrected its own mistakes, which reinforces that "use a stronger model" is not a full solution.

That is the current MABW claim boundary: **process accountability and traceability, not semantic proof**.

## What MABW Did Not Get Right

This run exposed real v0.7.x boundaries:

- A source link in the Claim Ledger says which source entry a claim is connected to. It does not prove the raw source supports every sub-claim.
- The Source Appendix is an entry point for questioning, not a certificate of factual correctness.
- Quality gates catch formatting, freshness metadata, reader residue, and some control issues, but they do not perform complete semantic fact-checking.
- The Auditor can audit the current ledger faithfully; if the ledger already overstates a source summary, the Auditor may faithfully preserve the problem.

Therefore, this organoid-industry brief is not an output-quality showcase and should not be delivered externally as a verified industry study.

## Fixes Converted Into Release Hardening

Three control-plane fixes were converted into pre-release hardening work:

1. **Audit artifact binding**

   `finalize` must reject stale audit reports if they still reference claim IDs absent from the current Claim Ledger, carry blocking findings, request repair, or contain stale ledger / brief binding metadata.

2. **Delivery semantics**

   README and delivery docs must state that the reader delivery files are `output/delivery/brief.md` and the configured DOCX. Source appendix content may be appended into those delivery files; standalone `output/source_appendix.md` is an audit/control copy, not an extra reader handoff file.

3. **Public export safety**

   Release consistency checks must run public-safety scanning to prevent local paths, token-like strings, environment file references, and configured private terms from entering public release surfaces.

## v0.8 Direction

This failure clarifies the first v0.8 priority:

- source entries need raw excerpt, `retrieved_at`, original URL, `source_tier`, `raw_excerpt_sha256`, and `source_kind`;
- sources with only model summaries and no raw excerpt should be discovery hints or summary-supported evidence, not strong evidence;
- support relations need graded values such as `explicitly_supported`, `raw_supported`, `summary_supported`, `directionally_supported`, `partially_supported`, `supportive_but_overextended`, `attribution_mismatch`, `needs_primary_source`, and `unsupported`;
- freshness and authority need to be evaluated on separate axes, so a fresh secondary summary cannot wash out an older authoritative primary source;
- high-risk phrases such as "first", "world first", "official", "approved", "national plan", "Phase 1", "acquisition", and financing amounts need stronger source tiers or explicit uncertainty markers;
- conjunctive claims should be split or marked for human review during Claim Ledger construction.

## Publication Boundary

This failure study is public because it clarifies MABW's boundary instead of hiding it.

Safe wording:

> A private organoid-industry research run produced a readable but factually overconfident brief. External review by ChatGPT 5.5 Thinking Max found that the main failure was not missing sources, but source-to-claim support calibration. MABW preserved enough intermediate artifacts to trace how the errors entered and propagated. This is why v0.8 focuses on source-to-claim semantic support evaluation.

Do not say:

> MABW proved output-quality improvement.

Do not say:

> Every claim is proven by the Claim Ledger.

The accurate version is:

> Every material claim links to a registered source entry: traceability, not yet semantic proof.
